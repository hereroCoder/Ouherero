History

The Ovaherero people migrated from East Africa to present-day Namibia around the 16th century, bringing vast herds of cattle, sheep, and goats with them as a pastoralist community. Their entire way of life revolved around these animals.

Cattle are central to their way of life, not just for sustenance but also as a symbol of wealth and status. They historically lived in communities organized around cattle herding, and their homes were often made using a mix of cow dung and mud.

Food

As pastoral farmers, the Herero's staple foods revolve around their livestock

Sour Milk

Omaere, a significant part of traditional Herero culture, is produced by women who handle the milking and acidification of milk. Girls are taught these techniques from an early age.

Tradtional Butter

The Herero produce their own butter, a traditional delicacy that enhances the flavor of many dishes. However, its production isn't a year-round activity. It occurs when cows are producing ample milk, usually during rainy seasons when the cows have sufficient food. During drought seasons, milk preservation for calves takes precedence, leading to reduced milking and less butter production.

Attire

The women's distinctive Victorian-style dress is known as "Ozonde". Ovaherero added a distinctive horn-like headscarf known as 'otjikaiva,' typically crafted from fabric matching the dress. This horned feature symbolizes the revered cattle, representing wealth and status within their communities.

Over time, the dress patterns have evolved, and the otjikaiva has taken on a more defined shape. These dresses have become a canvas for vibrant colors and incorporate various modern patterns, showcasing the Ovaherero's adaptation to contemporary influences while preserving their cultural identity.

Entertainment

Traditional dance

The "Omuhiva" dance is a beloved tradition among the Herero people, where men emulate the movements of cattle by stamping their feet, kicking up dust, and mimicking the upraised horns through swaying motions.

During the "Outjina", a female lead ties wooden pieces to her feet, creating captivating sounds while stamping. Others form a circle around the main dance, singing, and clapping. The lead dancer typically leads both the dance and the song.

Oviritje

Another facet of Ovaherero culture is Oviritje music. Oviritje has evolved over time. While initially performed without instruments, today, keyboardists, producers, and both male and female artists contribute to this genre.

In the early 2000s, groups like Opouua, ORemember, and Okazera were prominent, but solo artists have become more prevalent. People now commission songs for special occasions like weddings, birthdays, or graduations. However, older generations still tend to prefer traditional Herero hymns, otjina, and muhiva over Oviritje. These songs not only entertain but also impart life lessons and Herero sayings, preserving wisdom and cultural teachings through music.
